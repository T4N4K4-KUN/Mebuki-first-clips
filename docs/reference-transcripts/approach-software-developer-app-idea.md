### [0:00] Introduction

Host:
So, you have this amazing app idea and you need someone to build it, but every time that you try to approach a software developer with your app idea, they're running away from you like you have the plague. In this video, I'm going to cover how to approach software developers. First, we're going to talk about how to ask for an opinion, then idea validation, understanding the tech stack, and lastly, MVPs and prototyping. These are only my opinions, but let's get started.

### [1:00] Step 1: Ask for an Opinion, Not a Commitment

Host:
First, before even getting into the ideation stage, come to a developer and ask for their opinion to see if this thing is viable to build, rather than just asking them to join. An engineer can give you a reality check on what is technically possible and how long it's going to take. You cannot just say an AGI or a hoverboard is a good idea; you need an engineer to tell you if it's technically possible.

### [2:15] Step 2: Validate Your Idea First

Host:
Another thing you need to do is validate the idea before writing a single line of code. It's so simple nowadays to build a landing page with no-code tools like Squarespace, WordPress, or Canva. Put this in front of people to get validation and create some kind of demand. When you have solid evidence and traction, go back to the developer and say, "Hey look, I have this much interest, what do you think?".

### [3:45] Step 3: Checking the Developer's Skills

Host:
Asking someone to join your idea is making a life-changing decision; you spend more time with a business partner than you would spend with your family members. If the developer likes the idea, don't immediately jump on it. You need to see if this person has a proven portfolio and understand the concepts of front-end and back-end development. For your app to be successful as a two-man company, the developer needs both of these skills to build the app from scratch by themselves.

### [5:15] Step 4: Focus on the MVP

Host:
So you proved your idea and decided to partner up. At this stage, take all those cool features you planned and throw them on a shelf for now to focus on building an MVP. Determine the bare minimum for your app to function. Think of it like building a road: it just needs to be flat and allow vehicles to drive on it, while all the road signs and extra stuff don't belong in the MVP. Based on customer feedback, you can add extras later, but first, ask the developer how long the MVP will take to build.

### [6:45] Outro

Host:
Following these steps is not going to guarantee you find a partner because a developer might be occupied with different projects or lack the entrepreneurial spirit. But if you follow this, I guarantee it's going to be a hundred times easier to find a technical co-founder. We do have a Discord community where half of the members are software developers, so that might be a good place to give it a try. Thank you for watching and I'll see you in the next video, bye.

## Extracted Learnings

- Ask for a developer's opinion before asking for commitment.
- A developer can give a reality check on feasibility and time.
- Validate the idea before writing code.
- Use simple landing pages or no-code tools to test demand.
- Bring evidence or traction back to the developer.
- Check whether the developer has a proven portfolio.
- For tiny teams, full-stack ability matters.
- MVP means shelving cool extra features, defining the bare minimum needed for the app to function, and adding extras later based on customer feedback.
