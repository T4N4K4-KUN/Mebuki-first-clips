### [0:45] What is Acceptance Criteria?

Sierra:
The success of any project depends on the development team's ability to meet the client or customer's needs, which is why acceptance criteria is so important for product and development teams. When the acceptance criteria is clearly defined upfront, you can avoid issues later down the line. Acceptance criteria refers to a set of requirements that a product must meet in order to be accepted by a user, customer, or system. They are a set of pass/fail statements that define the success of the feature or product behavior from the end user's perspective.

### [1:25] Why is it Important?

Sierra:
Essentially, acceptance criteria helps you understand these two crucial questions: Are we building the right thing? And did we build it right?. Effective acceptance criteria is testable, clear and concise, easy to understand, and provides the end user's perspective. But why? Acceptance criteria serves several purposes for product and development teams. It helps you manage expectations, define scope, reduce ambiguity, establish testing criteria for QA, and defend against scope creep mid-sprint.

### [2:05] Who and When to Write It

Sierra:
Pretty much anyone on the team can write acceptance criteria. Sometimes it's defined and written by the product owner, the business or requirements analyst or the project manager, but the product owner or product manager should at least lead the conversation. They help make sure that the product requirements are written with customer needs in mind. We also recommend involving your cross-functional team members, including dev and your QA representatives, to help clearly communicate the vision and clear up any misalignment. So when should you write acceptance criteria? You should define it before development begins. Otherwise, you won't be able to accurately capture customer needs. However, if you define it too early, it can backfire. We suggest only writing acceptance requirements once you decide to move an item into the sprint backlog.

### [3:00] Formatting & Outro

Sierra:
All right, now that we understand acceptance criteria, let's talk about formatting. Acceptance criteria can be written in two common formats: "Given/When/Then" and "Verification List". The Given/When/Then style is similar to the traditional format of user stories, following this template: Scenario, Given, When, Then. First you explain the scenario, then given the context of the scenario, when a certain action is taken, you then explain the outcome of taking that action. If this scenario style format doesn't work for your situation, you'll want to use the Verification List style. It's very straightforward: all you need to do is define a bulleted list of pass/fail statements. At the end of the day, the format that you choose is flexible. ## Extracted Learnings

- Acceptance criteria define requirements a product must meet to be accepted by a user, customer, or system.
- They are pass/fail statements from the end user's perspective.
- They help answer whether the team is building the right thing and whether it was built right.
- Effective acceptance criteria are testable, clear, concise, easy to understand, and user-centered.
- They help manage expectations, define scope, reduce ambiguity, establish QA testing criteria, and defend against scope creep.
- They should be defined before development begins, but not too early.
- Common formats are Given/When/Then and Verification List.
- A simple bulleted list of pass/fail statements is acceptable when scenario format is too heavy.
